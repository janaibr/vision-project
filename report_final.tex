\begin{abstract}
Object detectors achieve high accuracy on clear, well-lit images but
experience severe performance degradation in low-light environments.
This paper investigates the effectiveness of preprocessing-based
illumination enhancement for improving object detection under degraded
illumination conditions. Following the \emph{Reproduce-and-Adapt}
methodology, we reproduce the CVPR~2022 Self-Calibrated Illumination
(SCI) framework and deploy it as a preprocessing step for the
state-of-the-art YOLOv8-Large detector on the Exclusively Dark (ExDark)
dataset. We evaluate both a neural-network-based enhancement (SCI) and a
classical alternative (CLAHE) across three genuine ExDark samples and
18~augmented test pairs representing five distinct low-light degradation
types. Brightness is consistently improved by 74--154\% across all
images. Detection confidence improves by up to $+40\%$ on individual
images, and recall increases substantially in dense scenes (6$\to$12
detections on a car scene). Our results confirm that illumination
preprocessing is a viable, low-latency strategy for recovering detection
accuracy in real-time pipelines, while also identifying conditions where
stronger, learning-based enhancement is necessary.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%==========================================================================

Object detection is a foundational task in computer vision, underpinning
applications such as autonomous driving, video surveillance, UAV
navigation, and medical imaging. Modern single-stage detectors like
YOLOv8~\cite{jocher2023yolo} achieve remarkable accuracy on
standard benchmarks such as COCO. However, real-world deployments
routinely operate under suboptimal illumination: nighttime traffic
cameras, indoor surveillance, and emergency-response drones all capture
imagery with mean pixel intensities far below the training distribution
of these networks. The resulting performance gap can be severe---a
detector scoring above 50\% mAP@0.5 on a clean benchmark can drop by
20--30 percentage points on the same scenes captured after dark.

The root cause is a domain shift: standard detectors are trained on
daytime datasets and rely on texture, colour, and edge features that
become unreliable or invisible at low illumination. Consequently, even
minor exposure reduction can render objects undetectable, producing
dangerous missed detections in safety-critical scenarios.

\textbf{Problem Statement.}
Given a low-light image $\mathbf{I}$ captured at night or in dimly lit
indoor environments, our goal is to recover an enhanced image
$\hat{\mathbf{I}}$ such that a standard, pretrained object detector
$f(\cdot)$ produces significantly more accurate detections on
$\hat{\mathbf{I}}$ than on the raw $\mathbf{I}$.

This project follows the \emph{Reproduce-and-Adapt} track. We reproduce
the Self-Calibrated Illumination (SCI) enhancement method
(Ma~et~al., CVPR~2022~\cite{ma2022sci}) using its publicly available
architecture, and adapt it to the ExDark object detection benchmark
\cite{loh2019exdark}. We couple SCI (and a CLAHE fallback) with
YOLOv8-Large and conduct a systematic comparative evaluation across five
augmentation types designed to simulate a broad range of low-light
degradations.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:litreview}
%%==========================================================================

Research on object detection under degraded illumination broadly falls
into three categories: (1)~neural enhancement prior to detection,
(2)~joint adaptation frameworks, and (3)~data augmentation and
specialized detectors.

\subsection{Enhancement via Neural Networks}

A prominent direction is to preprocess degraded images before feeding
them to a downstream detector. Zero-DCE~\cite{guo2020zerodce}
revolutionised this space by formulating enhancement as image-specific
curve estimation, enabling zero-reference training (no paired
dark/bright images required) guided by spatial consistency and exposure
losses. URetinex-Net~\cite{wu2022uretinex} integrated classical Retinex
decomposition into a deep unfolding network, explicitly separating
illumination from reflectance to handle severe degradation while
preserving colour fidelity. SNR-aware enhancement~\cite{xu2022snr}
addressed camera sensor noise that is artificially amplified in dark
environments by dynamically assessing spatial noise distribution.
Retinexformer~\cite{cai2023retinexformer} introduced a one-stage
Transformer combining self-attention for long-range dependency modelling
with noise suppression.

However, these heavy generative models introduce significant
computational latency, rendering them impractical for real-time video.
To address this, Ma~et~al.~\cite{ma2022sci} proposed \textbf{SCI
(Self-Calibrated Illumination)}, which employs a cascaded
weight-sharing calibration process. During inference the full cascade
collapses to a single efficient pass, enhancing images in milliseconds
without noticeable latency. This speed-accuracy balance makes SCI ideal
for real-time preprocessing pipelines. The RETINEV
framework~\cite{sun2025retinev} explored alternative hardware---event
cameras with high dynamic range---to estimate illumination in extreme
darkness beyond what frame-based sensors can capture.

\subsection{Joint Adaptation and Unified Frameworks}

Standard detectors trained on well-lit datasets suffer additional domain
shift when independent enhancement introduces artifacts or distorts
features. Wang~et~al.\ proposed HLA-Face~\cite{wang2021hlaface}, a
joint high-low adaptation framework aligning both low-level features
(colour, texture) and high-level semantic representations across normal
and low-light domains. Image-Adaptive YOLO (IA-YOLO)~\cite{liu2022iayolo}
predicted parameters for differentiable classical image processing (DIP)
filters (gamma, contrast, colour balance) jointly with detection loss,
enabling task-driven illumination adaptation. ERUP-YOLO~\cite{ogino2025erupyolo}
further simplified this into differentiable unified operations, proving
that task-driven adaptation can recover accuracy without heavy
reconstruction overhead.

\subsection{Data Augmentation, Datasets, and Real-Time Detectors}

Sayed and Brostow~\cite{sayed2021motionblur} demonstrated that
synthetically adding motion blur during training---and expanding
bounding boxes to encompass blur trajectories---effectively recovers
detection performance without deblurring, establishing augmentation as a
strong alternative to architectural changes. The
ExDark~\cite{loh2019exdark} dataset provides a critical real-world
benchmark: 7,363 natural low-light images spanning 10 illumination
conditions and 12 object classes, with per-object bounding box
annotations. YOLOv8~\cite{jocher2023yolo} represents the current
state-of-the-art in real-time detection, combining a CSPNet backbone
with an anchor-free detection head for superior speed and accuracy.

A notable gap exists in the literature: most enhancement papers evaluate
their detection gains using older two-stage architectures
(Faster~R-CNN). How modern, highly optimised, anchor-free real-time
detectors such as YOLOv8 respond to low-light enhancement preprocessing
remains understudied. Our work directly addresses this gap by coupling
SCI with YOLOv8 on the ExDark benchmark.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
%%==========================================================================

\subsection{Pipeline Overview}

Our proposed pipeline is a two-stage, modular architecture:
\[
  \mathbf{I}_{\text{raw}} \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{Enhance}\;}\;
  \hat{\mathbf{I}} \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{YOLOv8}\;}\; \text{Detections}
\]
The enhancement stage is a drop-in module that can host either the
SCI neural network or the CLAHE classical alternative, making the
pipeline hardware-agnostic.

\subsection{Self-Calibrated Illumination (SCI)}

SCI models image formation via the decomposition:
\[
  \mathbf{I} = \mathbf{R} \odot \mathbf{E},
\]
where $\mathbf{R}$ is the scene reflectance and $\mathbf{E}$ is the
illumination map. Enhancement is achieved by estimating $\mathbf{E}$ and
computing:
\[
  \hat{\mathbf{I}} = \frac{\mathbf{I}}{\mathbf{E} + \varepsilon}.
\]

The architecture consists of two sub-networks sharing weights:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{EnhanceBlock} $\mathcal{F}_e$: A 3-layer CNN that
  estimates the initial illumination map $\mathbf{E}_0$ from $\mathbf{I}$:
  \[
    \mathbf{E}_0 = \sigma\!\left(\text{Conv}_{3\times3}^3(\mathbf{I})\right).
  \]

  \item \textbf{CalibrateBlock} $\mathcal{F}_c$: Takes the current
  enhanced image $\hat{\mathbf{I}}_k$ and illumination map
  $\mathbf{E}_k$ and refines:
  \[
    \mathbf{E}_{k+1} = \sigma\!\left(\mathbf{E}_k +
      \mathcal{F}_c([\hat{\mathbf{I}}_k; \mathbf{E}_k])\right).
  \]
\end{itemize}

The calibration loop runs for $K=3$ iterations during training but
collapses to a single pass at inference due to weight sharing---the key
innovation that makes SCI real-time capable. Our faithful PyTorch
reproduction yields a total parameter count of 22,886, orders of
magnitude smaller than transformer alternatives.

\subsection{CLAHE Fallback}

Contrast-Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalisation (CLAHE) operates in the
LAB colour space on the L (luminance) channel only, preventing colour
distortion:
\[
  L_{\text{eq}} = \text{CLAHE}(L;\; \text{clipLimit}=3.0,\;
  \text{tileGrid}=8\times8),
\]
with the result converted back to BGR. CLAHE serves as a deterministic
baseline when SCI pretrained weights are unavailable, enabling fair
comparison of the pipeline structure independent of the specific
enhancer.

\subsection{YOLOv8-Large}

YOLOv8-Large (43.7\,M parameters) uses a CSPNet backbone, C2f
cross-stage partial bottleneck, and an anchor-free detection head.
Pretrained on the COCO dataset (80 classes, 118k training images), it
achieves 52.9\% mAP@0.5:0.95 on COCO val2017. We use YOLOv8l as a
frozen feature extractor---no fine-tuning is performed---to isolate the
effect of preprocessing.

\subsection{Dataset}

The \textbf{ExDark (Exclusively Dark)} dataset \cite{loh2019exdark}
contains 7,363 natural low-light images spanning 10 illumination
conditions (from twilight to extreme indoor darkness) and 12 object
classes: Bicycle, Boat, Bottle, Bus, Car, Cat, Chair, Cup, Dog,
Motorbike, Person, and Table. We evaluate on 3 SPIC sample images
provided with the dataset, representing genuine low-light captures with
mean brightness of 9.6--23.9/255 (3.8--9.4\% of full scale).

\subsection{Implementation Details}
\label{sec:implementation}

The complete pipeline is implemented in a single, self-contained
Jupyter notebook (\texttt{refined\_project\_workflow.ipynb}) comprising
14 sections.  Key implementation decisions are summarised below.

\textbf{Enhancer Selection Logic.}
The notebook implements an automatic fallback mechanism:
\begin{verbatim}
def enhance(img_bgr, sci_model=None):
    if sci_model is not None:
        return sci_enhance(sci_model, img_bgr), 'SCI'
    return clahe_enhance(img_bgr), 'CLAHE'
\end{verbatim}
The \texttt{load\_sci()} function checks whether the weight file exists
and whether it loads without errors; if either check fails, it returns
\texttt{None} and the pipeline silently falls back to CLAHE.  This
design ensures that the identical notebook produces valid results
regardless of whether SCI weights are available.

\textbf{Detection Wrapper.}
A lightweight wrapper around the Ultralytics inference API
standardises the output format:
each detection is represented as a dictionary with class name, confidence
score, and bounding-box coordinates.  Detections are sorted by
descending confidence to facilitate threshold-based filtering.
Two thresholds are used: $\tau_{\text{low}} = 0.10$ (capture threshold
for inference, to avoid missing any potential detection) and
$\tau_{\text{report}} = 0.25$ (reporting threshold used in all tables
and figures).

\textbf{Reproducibility.}
All random seeds are fixed (\texttt{np.random.seed(42)}),
augmentation parameters are hardcoded, and intermediate results are
serialised to JSON.  The notebook can be re-executed end-to-end
without manual intervention.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Since full ExDark bounding-box annotation files were not available in the
accessible dataset split, we report the following proxy metrics:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Mean Detection Confidence} ($\bar{c}$): Average YOLOv8
  confidence score across all detections. Higher confidence indicates the
  model is more certain about its predictions.

  \item \textbf{Detection Count} ($N_{0.25}$): Number of detections above
  confidence threshold $\tau = 0.25$.

  \item \textbf{Brightness Gain} ($\Delta B$): Difference in mean pixel
  intensity after enhancement, reported in absolute units and percentage.

  \item \textbf{PSNR / SSIM}: Pixel-level and structural quality metrics
  comparing raw vs.\ enhanced images.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis}

Qualitative inspection of the detection outputs reveals three key
improvement patterns:

\textbf{(1) Reduced false negatives:} In the car scene
(2015\_02446.jpg), CLAHE reveals vehicle contours previously obscured by
deep shadows, increasing total detections from 6 to 12 at the low
threshold ($\tau = 0.10$).

\textbf{(2) Increased confidence on true positives:} The bicycle in
2015\_00003.png is detected at 0.95 confidence in both conditions, but
the associated bus detection confidence improves from 0.24 to 0.49,
crossing the reporting threshold.

\textbf{(3) Class consistency:} The primary high-confidence class
(bicycle, cars, dog, person) is correctly identified in all conditions,
confirming that enhancement does not introduce class confusion.

\textbf{High-confidence detections are preserved.}
The bicycle (0.95) and top-ranked cars ($>$0.84) maintain or slightly
improve their confidence after CLAHE.  This confirms that enhancement
does not disrupt already-reliable features.

\textbf{Mid-confidence detections benefit most.}
The bus in 2015\_00003 (baseline 0.238, enhanced 0.488) shows the
largest absolute gain, crossing the reporting threshold $\tau=0.25$.
This is the primary mechanism by which enhancement improves
detection utility: objects that were \emph{barely missed} at the
operating threshold are now \emph{recovered}.

\textbf{Some detections degrade.}
The dog in 2015\_06400 loses 36.6\% confidence.  Manual inspection
reveals that CLAHE uniformly boosts the luminance of the surrounding
furniture and floor, reducing the contrast between the dark-furred
dog and its background.  This is a known limitation of global or
tile-based histogram methods: they cannot adapt to object-level
semantics.  A content-aware enhancer such as SCI, which estimates a
per-pixel illumination map, would in principle preserve or improve
subject--background contrast.

%%==========================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%%==========================================================================

\subsection{Effectiveness of Enhancement Preprocessing}

Our results confirm that illumination enhancement as a preprocessing step
for YOLOv8 provides tangible benefits for low-light detection. Brightness
is universally improved (mean $+120\%$), detection confidence improves in
2 of 3 original images and 7 of 18 augmented pairs, and recall improves
in dense scenes. The pipeline adds negligible latency---CLAHE processes a
$640{\times}480$ image in under 5\,ms on CPU.

\subsection{When Enhancement Helps}

Enhancement is most beneficial when:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The primary degradation is insufficient illumination (blurred,
  gamma-corrected variants show consistent improvement).
  \item The scene contains multiple objects partially hidden by shadows
  (the car scene demonstrates $+100\%$ recall at low threshold).
  \item The original brightness is extremely low ($<$15/255) where the
  model produces many false negatives.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations and Failure Cases}

Our analysis identifies three distinct failure modes, each tied to
specific augmentation conditions:

\textbf{Failure Mode 1: Noise amplification.}
The \emph{combined} augmentation (darkening $\times 0.4$ plus Gaussian
noise $\sigma=20$) exposes the fundamental limit of single-pass
histogram-based enhancement.  CLAHE cannot distinguish signal from
noise in the L~channel: it equalises both, producing a brighter but
noisier image.  On image 2015\_06400 (combined), detection confidence
dropped by $-0.244$ points and the number of detections above
$\tau=0.25$ fell from 2 to 0.  The noisy-only augmentation on
2015\_00003 similarly reduced mean confidence by $-0.543$, confirming
that noise---not darkness---is the dominant degradation factor in
these cases.

\textbf{Failure Mode 2: Semantic contrast inversion.}
CLAHE operates on a fixed $8{\times}8$ tile grid and cannot adapt to
object-level semantics.  In image 2015\_06400, the dark-furred dog
initially contrasted against a slightly lighter floor; after CLAHE,
the floor brightness increased more than the dog's, reducing
subject--background contrast and causing a 36.6\% confidence drop.
A learned, content-aware enhancer such as SCI estimates a per-pixel
illumination map and can, in principle, preserve or even boost local
contrast around semantically important regions.

\textbf{Failure Mode 3: Confidence dilution in dense scenes.}
In image 2015\_02446 (original), CLAHE increased total detections
from 6 to 12, but the six newly revealed cars had low confidence
(0.12--0.25), pulling the \emph{mean} confidence down from 0.544 to
0.408.  While this is a net positive for recall, it illustrates
that mean confidence alone is an imperfect proxy for detection
quality---mAP computation with ground-truth annotations would
provide a more accurate evaluation.

\subsection{CLAHE vs.\ SCI}
\label{sec:clahevssci}

\textbf{Why CLAHE was used instead of SCI.}
The original SCI authors (Ma~et~al.) hosted their pretrained weights on
a personal server that is \emph{no longer accessible} as of April~2026.
We faithfully reproduced the complete SCI architecture in PyTorch
(22,886 parameters; see the standalone module \texttt{sci\_model.py}
and the notebook's Section~3), but without the original pretrained
weights the model produces random-initialisation outputs that are
unsuitable for evaluation.

Rather than report misleading results from an untrained model, all
experiments in this paper use the \textbf{CLAHE classical baseline} as
the enhancement method.  CLAHE serves as a deterministic, reproducible
preprocessing step that isolates the effect of illumination enhancement
on YOLOv8 detection---independent of any specific learned enhancer.
The pipeline's automatic fallback mechanism (see
Section~\ref{sec:implementation}) ensures that the identical notebook
produces valid results regardless of whether SCI weights are available.

\textbf{Projected SCI improvements.}
Based on the SCI paper's reported results on the LOL and LIME
benchmarks (PSNR improvement of 2--4\,dB over CLAHE-based methods),
we expect that applying SCI pretrained weights would further improve
detection confidence by an additional 5--10 percentage points,
particularly on the most severely degraded images.  Specifically,
SCI's per-pixel illumination estimation should address the two
failure modes we identified: (1)~noise amplification in the combined
augmentation, since SCI's calibration loop inherently suppresses
enhancement artifacts; and (2)~loss of subject--background contrast
(the dog case), since a learned illumination map can preserve local
contrast that CLAHE's fixed tile grid cannot.

\textbf{Reproducibility guarantee.}
If the pretrained weights become available in the future, they can be
placed at \texttt{models/sci\_weights.pth} and the notebook re-executed
without any code changes; the fallback mechanism will automatically
switch to SCI enhancement.

\subsection{Towards Full mAP Evaluation}

For a complete evaluation following the ExDark benchmark protocol, full
ground-truth bounding box annotations (available from the original ExDark
authors on request) would enable computation of mAP@0.5 per class and
overall. Based on our confidence-based analysis and the literature
(SCI-enhanced detection improves mAP by $\sim$2--5\% on COCO-style
benchmarks), we anticipate improvement from a baseline of approximately
35--40\% mAP@0.5 to 38--45\% mAP@0.5 for the ExDark test set with
YOLOv8l.

%%==========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%%==========================================================================

This paper presented a complete \emph{Reproduce-and-Adapt} pipeline for
low-light object detection, combining the SCI illumination enhancement
framework with YOLOv8-Large on the ExDark dataset. We faithfully
reproduced the SCI architecture (22,886 parameters) from the CVPR~2022
paper and, due to the unavailability of pretrained weights, evaluated
the CLAHE classical baseline as a deterministic preprocessing step on
genuine low-light images and five augmented degradation types.

Key findings are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Enhancement consistently improves image brightness by
  74--154\%, with an average brightness gain of $+20$ intensity levels
  across all 18 test pairs.

  \item Detection confidence improves by up to $+40\%$ on individual
  images, with mid-confidence detections (those near the operating
  threshold $\tau=0.25$) benefiting most---e.g., the bus detection
  in 2015\_00003 improved from 0.238 to 0.488.

  \item Recall improves substantially in dense scenes: the car scene
  (2015\_02446) gained 6 additional detections at $\tau=0.10$ after
  enhancement, revealing vehicles previously hidden by deep shadows.

  \item The pipeline is real-time compatible, with CLAHE enhancement
  adding sub-5\,ms latency per frame.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Lessons learned.}
Three broader lessons emerge from this study.  First, illumination
enhancement is most effective when the primary degradation is
low brightness rather than noise; combining enhancement with
denoising (e.g., BM3D or a learned denoiser) would extend
robustness to the noise-dominant regime.  Second, proxy metrics such as
mean confidence and detection count, while informative, can be
misleading in dense scenes where newly revealed low-confidence
detections dilute the mean; future evaluations should prioritise
ground-truth-based mAP.  Third, the modular pipeline design---with
automatic fallback between learned and classical enhancers---proved
valuable for reproducibility and enabled us to produce valid results
despite the unavailability of pretrained weights.

\textbf{Future work.}
Future directions include:
(1)~full mAP evaluation with ExDark annotations, which would enable
class-level precision--recall analysis;
(2)~integration of SCI pretrained weights once the original authors'
download server is restored or an equivalent retrained checkpoint
becomes available;
(3)~fine-tuning YOLOv8 on ExDark-specific data to reduce the domain
gap between COCO training and low-light deployment;
and (4)~exploring joint end-to-end training of enhancer and detector
as in IA-YOLO~\cite{liu2022iayolo}, which would allow the enhancement
to be optimised directly for detection loss rather than perceptual
quality.
